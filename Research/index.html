  • Who is Sex-Selecting, and When?
    • Awarded Best Third Year Paper, NYU for AY 2020-2021

    Parents who prefer sons are known to engineer the sex-composition of their living children using prenatal sex-selective abortion and differential fertility stopping. When parents optimize over when to stop childbearing and if or when to sex-select, simple heuristics emerge that broadly describe parents' composition-engineering strategies given their preferences and birth histories. This paper identifies such a heuristic whereby son-preferring parents in India sex-select when they are at their ideal number of children so as to avoid exceeding it. Identification of fertility heuristics involve two components: (1) identifying instances of use of sex-selective methods, and (2) linking the use of sex-selective methods with parental preferences. To identify use of prenatal sex-selective methods, I compare inter-birth intervals preceding the birth at any order, since intervals preceding male births would be artificially longer than those preceding female births if sex-selective methods were used. To infer the heuristics, I check for the use of sex-selective methods at various birth-orders defined relative to the mother's ideal number of children. I find parents to typically sex-select when at the ideal number of children, even if means sex-selecting at the first birth order. I also find evidence of reversals in the decision to sex-select following unsuccessful attempts at select birth histories.

  • Groups and Interpersonal Behavior

    • Synthetic groups, created based on trivial and meaningless criteria, exhibit groupness, i.e. they favor their in-group relative to the out-group. This paper investigates why even in the absence of social ties or history, groups matter in interpersonal interactions. I examine the relevance of labeling in driving groupness (via group identity), against the alternative of being driven solely by strategic pecuniary interests (via expectations of generalized reciprocity). I experimentally manipulate payoff structures to show that subjects exhibit groupness even when their respective in-group cannot affect the subjects' payoff, maintaining that labeling is a significant source. To demonstrate if group labels hold any inherent significance for its members, I study subjects' reactions to how their synthetic in-group evaluates them. I ask synthetic groups to nominate two out of a total of four members for a bonus, and reveal the nomination results only to treated subjects. Subjects informed of being nominated exhibit more groupness by increasing their allocations to the in-group and decreasing allocations to the out-group. Those informed of their non-nomination decrease allocations to all and do not maintain any positive distinctness between their in-group and out-group, as if having dis-identified. Individuals therefore do attach importance to group labels more than just a salient dimension of categorization, and do so even in the absence of any pecuniary gains.
