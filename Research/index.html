Working Papers

     
  • Who are Sex-Selecting, and When?
    • Awarded Best Third Year Paper, NYU for AY 2020-2021

    Parents with a preference for sons are known to manipulate the sex-composition of their children using prenatal sex-selective abortion or continued childbearing until their desired number of sons are born. It is unlikely that parents consciously optimize a dynamic stochastic fertility problem when deciding whether and when to use such methods, but rather rely on simple heuristics. This paper identifies one such heuristic whereby son-preferring parents sex-select when they are at their ideal number of children so as to avoid exceeding it. Identification of the heuristic involves two components: (1) identifying instances of use of sex-selective methods, and (2) linking the use of such methods with fertility choices and parental preferences. To identify use of prenatal sex-selective abortion, I compare inter-birth intervals preceding male births, that would be artificially longer than those preceding female births if sex-selective methods were used. I then infer heuristics based on the use of such methods at various birth-orders defined relative to the mother's ideal number of children. In addition to the tendency to sex-select when at the ideal number of children, I also find evidence of reversals in the decision to sex-select following unsuccessful attempts at select birth histories.

  • Groupness among Synthetic Groups: Identity and Interpersonal Behavior

    • Synthetic groups created in the lab based on trivial and meaningless criteria also favor their "in-group" relative to the "out-group" (groupness). This paper investigates the psychological motive for this behavior. If it were that subjects begin to identify with their assigned group during the experiment, then receiving a poor evaluation from the in-group (acceptance threat to identity) should weaken such identification and decrease groupness. By asking groups to nominate two (out of four) of their members for a bonus, and experimentally manipulating the timing of the nomination status reveal, I find groupness to respond to nominations. Subjects informed of their nomination increase their allocations to the in-group and decrease allocations to the out-group. Those informed of their non-nomination decrease allocations to all and do not maintain any positive distinctness between their in-group and out-group, as if they have disidentified . To rule out expectation of generalized reciprocity as an alternate explanation for the apparent groupness, I find subjects' allocations to the in-group to be comparable under settings where the subjects' respective in-groups can and cannot affect their payoff. There is, however, significant heterogeneity in in-group favor as measured through the size of allocations made in modified dictator games.
